# NanoDAW Two Costumes Decision

Date: 2026-07-14
Status: provisional interaction verdict; listening confirmation required

## Decision

Keep the shared clock and transition ledger. Do not promote either disposable
interface into the persistent song model.

Use the **Session Deck as the control path** for the next listening session. It
made the next visual gesture more obvious with two prepared choices plus Stop.

Keep the **Mutation Instrument as the R&D challenger**. Its anchor, transpose,
rotate, and restore grammar is more distinctive and preserves reversible
causality, but it presents more decisions before the player has heard whether
that depth feels rewarding.

## What The Comparison Proved

- Both costumes share one 112 BPM, 4/4, next-bar clock contract.
- Both distinguish pending, scheduled, and observed state.
- Both replace or cancel a pending action and stop one track independently.
- Both return to a known state without opening the detailed editor.
- Neither writes live state into `Song`, history, or local persistence.
- Two fixed voices and matched material avoid a simple sound-quality bias.

## What It Did Not Prove

- sample-accurate or late-event scheduling;
- musical preference or desire for another run;
- final active-clip edit semantics;
- capture, recovery, recording, or scene semantics.

## Listening Gate

Run one uninterrupted pass per costume. While listening, record:

1. Which next gesture was obvious without reading every label?
2. Which change produced a coherent musical consequence?
3. Did Restore Anchor feel trustworthy?
4. Which costume invited an immediate second run?

Only that answer may promote a product grammar. Until then, the 4 x 4 launcher,
scenes, slots, recording, macros, Capture Jam, and rolling recovery remain
frozen.
